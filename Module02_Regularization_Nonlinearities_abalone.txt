A.1 Regularization Informed Best Subset Selection

The first required model used regularization to guide best subset selection. This approach follows the logic that shrinkage methods can reduce unnecessary complexity, while subset selection can then identify a smaller and more interpretable final regression model (James et al., 2023). In this dataset, the original design matrix contained nine predictors after the qualitative variable Sex was converted into indicator variables. Those predictors were length, diameter, height, whole_weight, whole_weight_1, whole_weight_2, shell_weight, sex_I, and sex_M.

The first stage of the analysis used the lasso as a screening method. This was an appropriate decision because the lasso is designed to shrink coefficient estimates toward zero, and unlike ridge regression, it can force some coefficients to become exactly zero. That property makes it a natural bridge between regularization and subset selection (James et al., 2023). The cross validated lasso curve showed that the minimum error occurred at alpha_min = 0.002206. The more conservative one standard error choice was alpha_1se = 0.016690. The plotted curve was fairly flat near the minimum, which means several penalty values produced similar predictive error. This is exactly the situation in which the one standard error rule is useful, because it supports choosing a simpler and more stable model when performance differences are very small (James et al., 2023).

The lasso screening results showed that at alpha_min all nine predictors remained active, but at alpha_1se only seven predictors remained. The retained variables were shell_weight, whole_weight_1, height, diameter, sex_I, whole_weight, and whole_weight_2. The removed predictors were length and sex_M, since their coefficients were shrunk to zero at the stronger penalty level. This is important because it shows that the lasso did not merely reduce coefficient size. It also performed actual variable screening by removing weaker predictors from the model. That result matches the course reading, which explains that the lasso differs from ridge regression because it can create sparse models that are easier to interpret (James et al., 2023).

The strongest screened predictor was shell_weight. Under the one standard error rule its coefficient was 3.006237. The next strongest signal was whole_weight_1 with a coefficient of negative 2.563760. Height and diameter also remained important positive signals, while sex_I remained a moderate negative signal. Whole_weight and whole_weight_2 were retained, but with much smaller effects. These results suggest that the variables most closely related to shell development and internal mass contributed more predictive information than length or the male indicator once the full predictor structure was considered.

After the screening stage, best subset selection was performed only on the seven retained predictors. This was efficient and statistically sensible because best subset selection becomes increasingly expensive as the number of candidate predictors grows, and the lasso had already reduced the search space to a smaller set of meaningful variables (James et al., 2023). The subset frontier showed clear improvement as model size increased. The one predictor model using shell_weight had a cross validated RMSE of 2.274518. The two predictor model reduced RMSE to 2.082444. The three predictor model reduced it further to 2.028107. The four predictor model reached 2.008498. The five predictor model achieved 1.999227. The six predictor model produced 1.994048, and the seven predictor model produced the lowest value at 1.988127.

Even though the seven predictor model had the smallest average cross validated RMSE, the final model was selected by the one standard error rule rather than by the absolute minimum alone. This rule selected the smallest model whose error was still within one standard error of the best point on the frontier. As a result, the chosen model had five predictors: shell_weight, whole_weight_1, height, diameter, and sex_I. This decision was fully aligned with the principle that when several models perform almost equally well, the simpler model is usually preferred because it is easier to explain and less likely to reflect minor noise in the data (James et al., 2023).

The selected best subset model performed well. On the training data, the model had an R² of 0.602080, an adjusted R² of 0.602052, an RMSE of 1.999003, and an MAE of 1.374227. On the validation data, the model had an R² of 0.595623, an adjusted R² of 0.595512, an RMSE of 2.038113, and an MAE of 1.400566. These results show that the model maintained similar explanatory strength and error levels when moving from the training sample to the holdout validation sample. That pattern supports the conclusion that the model generalized reasonably well and did not collapse outside the data used to fit it.

The coefficient estimates further strengthened the interpretation. Shell_weight had a coefficient of 24.625457 with a very large t value of 141.503928 and a p value of 0.000000. Whole_weight_1 had a coefficient of negative 13.298888 with a t value of negative 135.041462 and a p value of 0.000000. Height had a coefficient of 19.962148, diameter had a coefficient of 6.404928, and sex_I had a coefficient of negative 0.698711. All of these coefficients were statistically significant, and each 95 percent confidence interval excluded zero. This means the evidence supporting the retained predictors was extremely strong. In practical terms, larger shell weight, greater height, and larger diameter were associated with more rings, while higher whole_weight_1 and the infant indicator were associated with fewer rings after the other predictors were controlled.

The key conclusion for A.1 is that the first model successfully combined shrinkage and subset selection in a disciplined way. The lasso removed weak predictors and narrowed the variable pool, while best subset selection produced a five predictor regression model that was both interpretable and competitive in prediction. This directly reflects the distinction in the text between shrinkage methods, which regularize coefficients, and subset methods, which deliberately reduce the number of included predictors (James et al., 2023).

A.2 Principal Components Regression

The second required model was principal components regression. PCR is a dimension reduction method that first transforms the original predictors into a smaller set of orthogonal components and then regresses the response on those components instead of on the original variables (James et al., 2023). The main purpose of PCR is not variable elimination. Its purpose is to reduce dimensional instability by replacing the original correlated variables with uncorrelated component scores.

This was especially relevant in the Abalone dataset because several size and weight measurements are naturally related to one another. When predictors move together, ordinary least squares on the raw predictors can suffer from instability and severe collinearity. PCR addresses that issue by projecting the predictors into principal component space, where each component captures a different direction of variation and is orthogonal to the others (James et al., 2023). Before fitting PCR, the predictors were standardized. This was necessary because PCR depends on the scale of the predictors, and the assigned reading explains that standardization is generally recommended when the variables are not measured on identical scales (James et al., 2023).

The number of retained components was selected using 10 fold cross validation on the training data. The results showed that one or two components were not sufficient, since their cross validated RMSE values were 2.411052 and 2.411107. Performance improved slightly at three and four components, then improved sharply at five and six components. The six component model had a mean cross validated RMSE of 1.998958 with a standard error of 0.011579. Although the absolute minimum RMSE occurred at nine components with a value of 1.987971, the six component model still fell within one standard error of that minimum. Therefore, the one standard error rule selected six components as the final PCR structure. This choice was statistically appropriate because it balanced predictive performance and model simplicity, which is exactly the reasoning recommended in the assigned reading when the test error curve is relatively flat near its minimum (James et al., 2023).

The explained variance results support that decision strongly. The first principal component explained 79.6694 percent of the variance in the predictors. The second brought cumulative explained variance to 91.1995 percent. By the time six components were used, cumulative explained variance reached 99.2705 percent. This means that the six selected components preserved nearly all of the information in the original predictor space. Very little predictor variation was discarded. As a result, the six component PCR model achieved substantial dimensional compression without sacrificing much information.

The PCR model produced slightly stronger predictive results than the A.1 model. On the training data, PCR had an R² of 0.602195, an adjusted R² of 0.602162, an RMSE of 1.998713, and an MAE of 1.373681. On the validation data, PCR had an R² of 0.595830, an adjusted R² of 0.595696, an RMSE of 2.037591, and an MAE of 1.399946. These values were marginally better than those of the regularization informed best subset model. The difference was small, but the assignment requires a comparison supported by evidence and metrics, and the validation RMSE clearly identified PCR as the better of the two advanced models.

The coefficient table in component space also provides important interpretation. PC1, PC3, PC4, PC5, and PC6 were all highly significant, with p values equal to 0.000000. PC2 had a p value of 0.290219, which means it was not statistically significant in the component regression. However, because the model structure was chosen based on cross validated performance rather than on individual p values alone, retaining six components was still justified. This distinction matters because PCR is designed primarily as a predictive dimension reduction approach. It does not operate like best subset selection, where the final interpretation is attached directly to a small number of raw predictors (James et al., 2023).

To improve substantive interpretation, the PCR coefficients were also converted back to the original predictor scale. That table showed that shell_weight again had the strongest positive effect at 25.984724. Height also remained strongly positive at 20.525998. Diameter remained positive at 4.308959. Whole_weight_1 was strongly negative at negative 11.949376, and whole_weight_2 was also negative at negative 4.719046. The sex_I coefficient remained negative at negative 0.745887, while sex_M was nearly zero at negative 0.032191. These values tell a story very similar to the one observed in the A.1 model. The most influential physical characteristics were again shell related size and body dimension measures, while some internal weight measures moved in the opposite direction once the full model structure was accounted for.

The key conclusion for A.2 is that PCR successfully reduced the nine original predictors to six orthogonal components while preserving 99.2705 percent of the original predictor variation. It also slightly outperformed the regularization informed best subset model on the validation set. Most importantly, it solved the severe collinearity problem that remained in the A.1 model. Therefore, within the two required advanced models, PCR provided the strongest balance of predictive performance and statistical stability.

This is important because the assigned reading stresses that model selection should rely on validation or cross validation rather than on training fit alone, since training error is an optimistic estimate and does not provide reliable evidence of future performance (James et al., 2023).

A.4 Regression Assumptions, Best Subset Selection, Lasso Interpretation, and Overall Findings

The final assignment section required investigation of linearity, independence of errors, collinearity, and residual distribution. It also specifically asked for interpretation of best subset selection and the lasso. The assumption checks provide an important reality check because a model can have acceptable predictive accuracy and still violate several regression assumptions.

For the regularization informed best subset model, the linearity results were mixed. The Rainbow test had a p value of 0.979006, which suggests that the fitted linear relationship was broadly reasonable. However, the RESET test p value was 0.000000, which suggests that some nonlinear structure or omitted functional form may still remain. This mixed outcome is also visible in the residuals versus fitted plot, where the point cloud does not form a perfectly random band around zero. Instead, it shows some shape and spreading that indicate the model may not capture every pattern in the data.

The independence of errors assumption was supported. The Durbin Watson statistic for the best subset model was 1.994440, which is very close to the ideal value of 2. This suggests there was little evidence of serial correlation in the residuals. The PCR model showed the same strength, with a Durbin Watson statistic of 1.994625. Therefore, independence was one of the strongest assumption results for both models.

Homoscedasticity was not supported in either model. The Breusch Pagan p value was 0.000000 for the best subset model and also 0.000000 for PCR. The scale location plots reinforce that conclusion because the spread of the standardized residuals increases across parts of the fitted range rather than remaining constant. This means the error variance changed across prediction levels. In practice, that does not make the models useless, but it does mean the regression errors are not equally dispersed and the standard inferential assumptions are not fully satisfied.

Normality of residuals was also not supported in either model. For the best subset model, the Jarque Bera p value and the Shapiro Wilk p value were both 0.000000. The residual skewness was 1.462654 and the kurtosis was 9.658912. For PCR, the Jarque Bera and Shapiro Wilk p values were also both 0.000000. Its skewness was 1.450278 and its kurtosis was 9.723609. These values indicate positive skewness and heavy tails. The normal Q Q plots confirm this visually because the points deviate strongly from the diagonal line, especially in the tails. Thus, neither model satisfied the normal residual assumption.

The largest difference between the two models appeared in the collinearity results. For the best subset model, the VIF table showed severe multicollinearity. Diameter had a VIF of 97.349244. Height had 96.857233. Shell_weight had 32.369732, and whole_weight_1 had 24.607574. Only sex_I had a VIF at a more acceptable level, with 2.176087. This means the retained raw predictors were strongly related to one another, which can make coefficient estimates unstable and difficult to interpret with confidence. The condition number of 90.246902 also supports the same conclusion.

In contrast, the PCR model completely resolved the collinearity issue in component space. Every retained component had a VIF of 1.000000. The condition number dropped to 9.845071. This is a major advantage of PCR because orthogonal principal components remove the instability caused by strongly correlated original predictors (James et al., 2023). Therefore, even though PCR was only slightly better in prediction, it was clearly stronger in terms of coefficient stability and design matrix structure.

Both models also flagged many influential and high leverage observations. The best subset model had 4,380 observations with Cook’s distance greater than 4 divided by n and 5,063 observations with leverage greater than 2p divided by n. The PCR model had 4,517 Cook’s distance flags and 4,367 leverage flags. These numbers may seem large, but they must be interpreted in the context of a very large training sample and extremely small cutoffs. The leverage and Cook’s distance plots show that most observations had very low leverage, while a limited number of cases sat well away from the main cloud. These points deserve attention because they can influence coefficient estimation more than ordinary cases.

The lasso interpretation is also central to this section. The lasso performed exactly the function that the reading assigns to it. It used an L1 penalty to shrink weaker coefficients to zero and retain the stronger ones. At the minimum error penalty the lasso preserved all nine predictors, but at the one standard error penalty it retained only seven. This is consistent with the idea that when prediction error is nearly unchanged across several penalty values, the more regularized and simpler solution is often preferable (James et al., 2023). The lasso therefore provided a sparse screening result that reduced complexity and guided the later subset search. Its most important retained signals were shell_weight, whole_weight_1, height, diameter, and sex_I. That screening result gave the best subset stage a more focused starting point.

Best subset selection also performed its intended role well. Instead of keeping every screened predictor, it evaluated the best model at each subset size and then used cross validated RMSE plus the one standard error rule to select the final size. The resulting five predictor model was not the absolute minimum error model, but it was the smallest structure that still lay within one standard error of the best frontier point. That is a strong textbook example of selecting a model based on estimated test performance rather than on training R² or training RSS alone (James et al., 2023). The subset frontier plot also made this decision visually persuasive because improvement from five to seven predictors was very small.

The final comparison leads to a balanced conclusion. The regularization informed best subset model was the more directly interpretable model because it expressed the final regression in terms of five original predictors. The PCR model was the more statistically stable model because it removed the severe collinearity problem and slightly improved validation RMSE. The difference in predictive accuracy was small, but it was enough to recommend PCR as the preferred submission among the two advanced models. The validation RMSE was 2.037591 for PCR and 2.038113 for the best subset model. The cross validated RMSE used during selection also favored PCR slightly, with 1.998958 compared with 1.999227 for the subset model.

The most important overall finding is that both advanced models clearly improved over the null model and produced meaningful prediction of Rings, but neither model satisfied every regression assumption. Independence was acceptable, while normality, constant variance, and some aspects of linearity required caution. The best subset model also suffered from severe collinearity, which PCR corrected very effectively. For that reason, PCR was the stronger final recommendation. At the same time, the lasso and best subset sequence was highly valuable because it showed how regularization can guide feature screening and then produce a smaller, interpretable, and empirically competitive regression model.
